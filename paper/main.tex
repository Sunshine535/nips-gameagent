\begin{abstract}
Large language model post-training typically optimizes a single alignment objective and does not explicitly develop strategic reasoning capabilities.
We introduce \textbf{GameAgent}, a unified game-theoretic self-play framework that simultaneously improves both strategic reasoning and multi-objective alignment.
Our approach comprises two complementary tracks:
(A)~\textbf{GRPO self-play} across 10 diverse strategic games---spanning matrix games, N-player games, and sequential games---to develop emergent strategic reasoning through competitive and cooperative dynamics;
(B)~\textbf{Nash-DPO}, a formally grounded multi-objective alignment method based on Nash bargaining theory that adaptively balances correctness, safety, efficiency, and creativity objectives.
Through a factorial experimental design (Base, SFT-only, A-only, B-only, A+B) on Qwen3.5-9B, we demonstrate that:
(1)~game-theoretic self-play significantly improves strategic reasoning, with agents converging toward Nash equilibrium strategies;
(2)~Nash-DPO achieves Pareto-superior multi-objective alignment compared to uniform-weight DPO;
(3)~the two tracks exhibit complementary improvements, with the combined A+B configuration outperforming either track alone on both game-theoretic metrics and standard NLP benchmarks.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%====================================

Current large language model (LLM) post-training methods face two fundamental limitations.
First, alignment is typically treated as a single-objective optimization problem---maximizing helpfulness subject to safety constraints---which inadequately captures the multi-dimensional nature of human preferences across correctness, safety, efficiency, and creativity~\citep{rame2024rewarded,wu2024fine}.
Second, standard RLHF and DPO procedures do not develop \emph{strategic reasoning}: the ability to reason about other agents' beliefs, intentions, and likely actions in interactive settings~\citep{gandhi2024strategic,duan2024gtbench}.

Game theory provides a natural framework for addressing both limitations simultaneously.
Competitive and cooperative game dynamics require agents to reason strategically about opponents, while Nash bargaining theory~\citep{nash1950bargaining} offers a principled solution for multi-objective optimization that satisfies individual rationality, Pareto optimality, and symmetry axioms.

We introduce \textbf{GameAgent}, a unified framework that leverages game-theoretic self-play for LLM post-training.
Our approach consists of two complementary tracks:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Track A: GRPO Self-Play} trains LLM agents through self-play across 10 diverse strategic games, using Group Relative Policy Optimization~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath} with iterative opponent updates. This develops emergent strategic reasoning that transfers across game types.
    \item \textbf{Track B: Nash-DPO} applies Nash bargaining theory to multi-objective alignment, where four objective agents (correctness, safety, efficiency, creativity) negotiate through an iterative DPO framework with dynamically computed Nash bargaining weights.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{unified game-theoretic framework} that simultaneously improves strategic reasoning and multi-objective alignment through self-play.
    \item \textbf{Nash-DPO}: a formally grounded multi-objective DPO method based on Nash bargaining (Definition~\ref{def:moag}, Proposition~\ref{prop:nash_dpo}).
    \item \textbf{Cross-game transfer}: the first systematic study showing that diversity of game environments drives emergent strategic reasoning capabilities.
    \item \textbf{Factorial analysis}: A-only, B-only, and A+B configurations reveal complementary improvements, validated through factorial ANOVA interaction tests.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%====================================

\paragraph{Self-Play for LLMs.}
Self-play has emerged as a promising paradigm for LLM improvement.
SPIN~\citep{chen2024self} uses self-play to generate training data from the model itself.
SPAG~\citep{cheng2024self} applies self-play in adversarial games.
SPIRAL~\citep{yuan2024self} combines self-play with iterative refinement.
Our work extends these approaches to a broader set of strategic games with formal game-theoretic grounding.

\paragraph{Multi-Objective Alignment.}
Several works address the multi-objective nature of alignment.
Reward Soups~\citep{rame2024rewarded} interpolate between reward-specific models.
MORLHF~\citep{wu2024fine} applies multi-objective reinforcement learning.
Pareto RLHF explores Pareto-optimal solutions.
Our Nash-DPO differs by providing formal Nash bargaining guarantees.

\paragraph{Game Theory in Machine Learning.}
GANs~\citep{goodfellow2014generative} pioneered game-theoretic training.
Multi-agent RL has explored Nash equilibrium learning~\citep{hu2003nash}.
MAgICoRe~\citep{she2024magicore} applies game-theoretic reasoning to code generation.
Nash-MD~\citep{munos2024nash} uses mirror descent for RLHF.
Our work uniquely combines strategic reasoning development with multi-objective alignment.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%====================================

\subsection{Game-Theoretic Self-Play Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

We design 10 game environments spanning three categories:
\textbf{(i)} symmetric 2-player matrix games (Prisoner's Dilemma, Coordination Game, Battle of the Sexes, Stag Hunt, Chicken, Matching Pennies);
\textbf{(ii)} N-player games (Public Goods, Auction); and
\textbf{(iii)} sequential games (Ultimatum, Negotiation).
Each game is presented to the LLM through a structured prompt containing the game rules, available actions, payoff structure, and interaction history.

%----------------------------------
\subsection{Track A: GRPO Self-Play for Strategic Reasoning}
\label{sec:grpo}
%----------------------------------

\paragraph{SFT Warmup.}
We first train 4 LoRA agents on supervised fine-tuning data generated by prompting the base model to play each game for 500 episodes.
Each agent is assigned 2--3 games and trained for 2 epochs, providing a warm start for self-play.

\paragraph{Iterative Self-Play.}
Over $T=3$ iterations, agents play all 10 games against each other.
In each iteration, we generate 2000 episodes per game, with agents alternating roles.

\paragraph{GRPO Update.}
For each episode, we compute group-relative advantages:
\begin{equation}
\hat{A}^{\text{group}}(s, a) = \frac{R(s,a) - \mu_{\text{group}}}{\sigma_{\text{group}} + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{\text{group}}$ and $\sigma_{\text{group}}$ are computed over a group of $K$ sampled responses.
The policy update minimizes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}} = -\mathbb{E}\left[\min\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a|s)}{\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)}\hat{A}, \text{clip}\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a|s)}{\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)}, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon\right)\hat{A}\right)\right] + \beta D_{\text{KL}}[\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}}]
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Cross-Game Transfer.}
To assess emergent strategic reasoning, we evaluate zero-shot transfer: training on $K$ games and evaluating on the remaining $10-K$ games without additional fine-tuning.

%----------------------------------
\subsection{Track B: Nash-DPO for Multi-Objective Alignment}
\label{sec:nash_dpo}
%----------------------------------

\begin{definition}[Multi-Objective Alignment Game]
\label{def:moag}
We define a cooperative game $\mathcal{G} = (N, \{u_i\}, D, \pi_{\text{ref}})$ where:
$N = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ is the set of $n$ objective agents (correctness, safety, efficiency, creativity);
$u_i : \Pi \to \mathbb{R}$ are agent utility functions over the policy space $\Pi$;
$D = (u_1(\pi_{\text{ref}}), \ldots, u_n(\pi_{\text{ref}}))$ is the disagreement point; and
$\pi_{\text{ref}}$ is the reference SFT policy.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Nash-DPO Objective]
\label{prop:nash_dpo}
The Nash bargaining solution for the multi-objective alignment game, combined with the DPO reparameterization, yields:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Nash-DPO}}(\pi) = -\sum_{i \in N} w_i \cdot \log \sigma\!\left(\beta\left[\log\frac{\pi(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \log\frac{\pi(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right]\right)
\label{eq:nash_dpo}
\end{equation}
where the Nash bargaining weights are computed as:
\begin{equation}
w_i^{(t)} \propto \frac{1}{u_i(\pi^{(t-1)}) - u_i(\pi_{\text{ref}}) + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Agents furthest from their ideal outcome receive higher weight, ensuring balanced multi-objective optimization.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:nash_dpo} presents the iterative procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Iterative Nash-DPO}
\label{alg:nash_dpo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, objective agents $\{u_i\}_{i=1}^n$, iterations $T$, DPO $\beta$
\STATE Initialize $\pi^{(0)} \leftarrow \pi_{\text{ref}}$
\FOR{$t = 1, \ldots, T$}
    \STATE Each agent generates candidate responses and cross-evaluates
    \STATE Compute marginal utilities: $\delta_i = u_i(\pi^{(t-1)}) - u_i(\pi_{\text{ref}})$
    \STATE Compute Nash weights: $w_i^{(t)} \propto 1 / (\delta_i + \epsilon)$
    \STATE Aggregate preference pairs with weights $\{w_i^{(t)}\}$
    \STATE DPO update: $\pi^{(t)} \leftarrow \arg\min_\pi \mathcal{L}_{\text{Nash-DPO}}(\pi^{(t-1)})$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\pi^{(T)}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Robust Reward Functions.}
Each objective agent employs a robust reward function designed to resist reward hacking:
\textbf{Correctness}: NLI entailment (DeBERTa-v3-large) combined with semantic cosine similarity;
\textbf{Safety}: multi-level regex pattern detection with severity weighting;
\textbf{Efficiency}: information density = coverage $\times$ (1 hBc$ length\_penalty);
\textbf{Creativity}: windowed type-token ratio (TTR) plus sentence variety score.
All rewards include anti-hacking penalties for excessive length and n-gram repetition.


%----------------------------------
\subsection{Factorial Experimental Design}
\label{sec:factorial}
%----------------------------------

To isolate the contribution of each track, we evaluate five conditions in a factorial design:
\textbf{Base} (unmodified Qwen3.5-9B),
\textbf{SFT-only} (supervised fine-tuning on game data),
\textbf{A-only} (GRPO self-play),
\textbf{B-only} (Nash-DPO),
\textbf{A+B} (GRPO followed by Nash-DPO).
We test for complementarity via the interaction effect in factorial ANOVA.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%====================================

We introduced GameAgent, a unified game-theoretic self-play framework for LLM post-training.
Through GRPO self-play across 10 diverse games and Nash-DPO for multi-objective alignment, we demonstrated that game-theoretic training simultaneously improves strategic reasoning and produces Pareto-superior multi-objective alignment.
The factorial experimental design confirmed that these two tracks provide complementary improvements.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our experiments use a single model scale (9B parameters), proxy reward functions rather than human judgments, and English-only game environments.

\paragraph{Future Work.}
Scaling to larger models (70B+), incorporating real safety classifiers, extending to more complex multi-step games, and exploring multi-language settings.
